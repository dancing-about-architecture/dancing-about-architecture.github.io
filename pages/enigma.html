Enigma – Mea Culpa

There are many who, justifiably, point to this album as being the first true ambient dub disc. And whether it’s true or not, this was certainly the first one to really catch on and it remains today enormously influential to the whole scene, having set a lot of the conventions that still remain in effect. It is remarkable to me that this innocuous album could’ve been at all controversial, but there was a brief flurry of protest when it was released in 1990. Apparently, the union of Gregorian chant with spoken excerpts from the Marquis de Sade and suggestive moaning and come hither production alarmed some of those upstanding citizens who feel the need to be constantly alarmed about the moral decay in our society. And to sound that alarm directly in our ears. Listen to this, the argument seems to go, and it’s but a couple of slippery steps down to raping nuns. Personally, I think the sacred and profane belong together. What is sex, if not simultaneously sacred and profane?

At any rate, Enigma was the first group to discover the seductive qualities of putting bits of Gregorian chants with shimmery, silky beats, and lush, velvety production. Ambient chants have become a bit cliché at this point, but it was virgin territory at the time. In fact, Enigma blossomed from a microscene of ambient Gregorian chant dub, captured on the obscure German compilation Real Sadeness and featuring such never-to-be-heard-from-again groups as Nostradomus, Ars Mundi, and Magna Charta. This scene, and its superstar, Enigma, anticipated the global fascination with Gregorian chant that would explode a few years later with the surprisingly popular chant collection (called, cleverly enough, Chant), by the Monks of Santo Domingo de Silos. I’m sure a long and interesting treatise could be written about why, on the verge of the millennium, the world was suddenly fascinated by a musical form previously popular almost 1500 years ago, and I’m equally sure that I’m not the one to write it. At least not here and now.

Mea Culpa is a bit too energetic to be strictly ambient dub, but it does showcase all of the trademark Enigma moves that later became industry-wide standards: the chant at the beginning, the mysterious vocal bites (bonus points for being in French), the dripping sensuality (and suggestive panting), the faux-shakuhachi melody (that’s the flute with the hiccup at the end – which, thanks to sampling, has become the sound of the remarkably versatile shakuhachi, rendering the rest of its tonal palette completely mute to most ears). About the only this missing is dripping water and the sound of planes flying over head, but don’t worry, they get to that later.

This first release was the most successful for the group, but they’re still going strong. Like Deep Forest, their subsequent releases have incorporated (or exploited, depending on you point of view) many other exotic musical traditions, with mixed results. But this isn’t really music you’re supposed to listen to, it’s just supposed to be there, perfuming the room, whispering sweet nothings, pulling you and your loved one inexorably towards the sacred profanity of your bed.
