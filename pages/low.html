Low – Do You Know How to Waltz?

Low is a group that I’d heard about for a long time, usually associated with other bands like Tortoise or Banyan who are often grouped into a category called post-rock, whatever that might mean. Although I had heard about them for a while, my brief experience with other post-rock bands (i.e. Banyan blows) made me reticent about buying one of their albums. Then, one day, I went on a buying binge at Aron’s Records – something that I do once every year or so. I ran across a collection of Low songs remixed by other musicians and, for whatever reason, decided to take a chance. Remix collections, of which there are plenty, are a companion to the more traditional tribute album in which different musicians get together and cover some honored artist’s songs. In the remix tribute, the original studio tracks are lent to a producer/DJ type and they have their way with the source material. Like the more traditional tribute album, the finished tracks often reveal more about the tributer than the tributee, but that can be part of the fun. The downside (or the upside, depending on your perspective) is that the remixed tracks often bear scant resemblance to the original tracks. Thus, one might not get an accurate picture of Steve Reich’s music by listening only to his remix tribute (Reich Remixed). Such is the case with the Low remixes, from an album titled Owl (get it? – Low remixed!). Many of the tracks are very interesting, with unusual textures and phrasing, but have bear little resemblance to their original source. There’s a (probably apocryphal) story about Aphex Twin, an absurdly prolific electronica artist who has been commissioned to do probably hundreds of remixes. One day, some band showed up to collect their remix from him, something for which he’d already been paid. Only problem was, he’d forgotten all about it. So he reached over into his gigantic pile of unissued tracks, pulled one out at random, and handed it to them. They loved it. Like I say, probably not true, but you get the idea of how far afield a remix can go.

Anyway, I chose this track, Do You Know How to Waltz, because I like the strange loops and the tinkling piano and the fact that this is ostensibly supposed to be a waltz and it’s written in 4/4 time. Reminds me of this terrible track the Pet Shop Boys did that was supposed to be in 3/4, but was constructed by merely chopping a beat out of 4/4 drum pattern, which gave it a really awkward feel. I’m sure they could try to defend such an action by saying the awkwardness is intentional, but I think it’s just because they’re hacks – a 3/4 phrase is much different than a 4/4 phrase with a note cut off.

I liked Owl enough that, years later, I finally tracked down a couple of original Low albums and bought them. I slipped I Could Live in Hope into my car’s CD player and hit the road for my morning commute. It was a cool, grey morning, and I was in a cool, grey mood, and I couldn’t have picked a better disc for the journey. Low, like their name, album covers, and location (Minnesota) suggest, is a sparse, slow, sad group, the aural equivalent of a twilight snow in a blue forest. Very reminiscent of the wonderful (and wonderfully named) Cowboy Junkies, their music stretches slowly and deliberately across the pale sky, sparse with peaceful remorse. Since I was fighting a tenuous battle with depression at the time, I found the slow syrup songs very sympathetic, and found I couldn’t get the CD out of my car for a couple of weeks. Finally, I could take no more, and popped it out for a change of scenery. I was tempted to start listening to the other disc of theirs I’d picked up – Long Division – but I knew that would be too much blue for me to take in my fragile state and I wasn’t ready to go completely under the ice yet. Someday, perhaps, but right now, if I go too Low, I’ll hit the bottom, and I don’t have the energy for that soul-weary climb back up into the sun.
