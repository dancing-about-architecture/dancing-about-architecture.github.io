Coldcut – Timber

There are precious few good band names. Most range from the absurd (Everything But the Girl) to the nonsensical (Orchestral Manouevers in the Dark). There are self-contradictory band names (Led Zeppelin, Iron Butterfly). There are bands named after people who aren’t in them (Lynard Skinnard, James) and bands named for places in which the members do not live (Bay City Rollers, Idaho, Nazareth). There is a plethora of too-clever-by-half names (The The, The Who – in fact, at one point the Who was in the Guinness Book of World Records for being the loudest rock band. Stumbling across this fact while in the company of my friend Mel, I asked her to guess the name of the loudest rock band. “I don’t know, who?” “Yes.” “Yes?” “No, not Yes.” Well then, who?” “Yes!” “Yes?” “No, not Yes, Who!” and so on (cut to Abbot and Costello, spinning in their graves)).

We quickly get used to these names and no longer see them for the travesties they are (The Beatles?), but a very few bands manage to come up with a good solid name, and most don’t even bother to try. My theory about why The Rolling Stones have survived for as long as they have is they have just about the best name you can have for a band (it certainly doesn’t have anything to do with the quality of their music). You know, gather no moss, rock ‘n’ roll, rolling your own and getting stoned, and so on. Of course, if all it took to succeed was a good name, than The Hitmen and Phil ‘n’ The Blanks would be on top of the charts. But, needless to say, they’re not.

Coldcut has to be one of the best possible names for a DJ outfit. Not only does it bring to mind delicatessens and the slathering of different elements (meats, beats) together to create a new tasty food (sandwich, dance hit), but it also references the cool way the DJ’s manipulate their cuts and scratches.

Coldcut was one of the first DJ outfits to be recognized as artists in their own right, spawning British DJ culture along with Bomb the Bass (another terrible name). Over the years, they’ve become sort of the godfathers of the scene, creating a collective (DJ Food – which isn’t so much a name but a description of what they were producing) and starting one of the best dance labels there is – the supremely funky, always interesting Ninja Tunes. And through it all, they’ve been releasing their own wonderfully accomplished material – although not as often as some of us might like.

This track comes from their most recent full-length release, Let Us Play (actually, the entire disc was remixed (and wonderfully so) and released as Let Us Replay, which would make that the most recent full-length release). Coldcut follows a socialist agenda, but doesn’t let their politics interfere with their mission to shake yer booty (for the most part, anyway – the track featuring ex-Dead Kennedy frontman Jello Biafra (there’s a couple of good names right there) ranting about the loss of personal freedom gets a bit bogged down, no matter how timely the message is). Although you wouldn’t necessarily know it, this track is a slam to the voracious timber industry that is currently raping our planet at an alarming rate. With the exception of the ubiquitous (thanks to Deep Forest) stolen pygmy sample and some of the drones, all of the sounds on this track are taken from trees being cut down and processed into timber – a fact made stunningly clear on the phenomenal video for this song, which is included on the CD. This track shows their highly refined skills in coaxing funky beats out of the most unlikely sources, and is one of the things that makes them one of the more interesting and accomplished DJ outfits out there.

Coldcut made an early name for themselves with their seminal remix of Eric B and Rakim’s Paid in Full, which features, among other elements, a vocal sample from Israeli singer Ofra Haza that inspired a thousand bedroom knob twiddlers (and I mean that in a nice way) like Banco de Gaia’s Toby Marks to broaden their horizons and include ethnic elements in their music. One of Coldcut’s lasting contributions to DJ culture is their total disregard for artificially imposed musical categories and their willingness to put whatever they like into their mixes, and not just rely on the same stale James Brown samples that are officially sanctioned by the hip hop culture cops. This is best shown on their legendary contribution to the ongoing Journeys by DJ series called 70 Minutes of Madness, in which they freely mix hip hop and jungle and ambient with dialogue from badly translated kung-fu movies and bits of the Dr Who theme. Highly recommended.
