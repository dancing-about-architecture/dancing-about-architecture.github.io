Lanterna – Silent Hills

I vowed I would stop buying CDs once my collection became unwieldy. Once I no longer knew what was in it, I would know it had grown to be too big and I would stop. But what does it mean to know your collection? Does it mean you know all the songs in it by heart? Or does it mean you can at least recognize what anything is, even if you can’t come up with a title or sing any of the lyrics? Because that hasn’t been true for quite a while. Once, in high school, my two best friends and I played Name That Tune by blindly pulling records out, putting the needle down, and challenging the other two to be the first to identify the track. Back then, my collection barely spanned a hundred albums, and back then, I really knew my collection. I was once able to name a tune by the sound of the surface noise preceding it – no actual notes had yet been played.

But those days are long since gone. Now it’s very easy for somebody to pull something out of my collection and play it for me and I will have no idea what it is. I find this happens naturally all the time. I used to make about one mixed tape per month, just to mark the passage of time and to familiarize myself with my new purchases. Unfortunately, I had the bad habit of not labeling any of these tapes. The logic was that I’d make the tape, then listen to it to make sure it was alright, then I’d write the songs down on the case once I was sure of it. The problem was, I’d never get to that last step. So there are a couple of dozen of unlabelled tapes lying around my house and every now and then I’ll pick one up and play it and I’ll run across some cool track and have absolutely no idea who it is or where it came from – especially since I listen to a fair amount of electronica where such obvious signposts as lyrics and singing styles don’t come into play.

So, an argument could certainly be made that I no longer know my collection. But those guidelines are too restrictive, and would prohibit almost any collection of any size. So I decided that I would stop buying new music if I ever unwittingly purchased the same CD twice. Then I’d know I had a problem.

KCRW used to play the mellow, dreamy guitarscapes of Lanterna quite frequently for a while, and I got hooked. The CD I was eventually able to track down was part of such a limited edition that it looked handmade. The disc itself was trapped inside this (irritatingly) elaborate cardboard jacket that unfolded into a couple of pieces before you could get to the music. Charming. But the treasure inside is well worth the hassle.

Lanterna is a guitar-based instrumental outfit from Champaigne, Illinois. Their music is layered and expansive and repetitive and darkly glorious. This particular track is a little more heroic than the rest, but it shows how this mysterious artist (group? musician?) is able to infuse emotion and passion into an ambient idiom – something that is far too rarely encountered. There is one track on this disc that’s pretty abrasive, but the rest of it is like this – perfect music for the end of a party, when the last few guests are lingering and the last few dishes are being gathered up after the social frenzy and everybody’s stretching and yawning and thinking of bed.

I love this album, and always kept an ear out for more from them, but nobody knew who they were or where they came from and they disappeared just as mysteriously as they had arrived, in a veil of fog.

Then, two years later, I happened to see a new Lanterna CD in the racks at Aron’s, my main musical dealer. It was brightly packaged in a normal CD case and had beautiful color graphics on both sides, where the rare original had monochromatic printing on grey cardboard. I snapped it up and rushed home only to discover that it was the same album I already had, only repackaged by some company that had acquired it. Because all the songs are instrumentals, I didn’t recognize any of the titles, and because there was so little other written information on either outer sleeve, I had no other way of knowing it was the same album. How could I?

So, even though I did the deed that was supposed to break my collection, I was duped into doing it. So it doesn’t count. Besides, I gave it away.
