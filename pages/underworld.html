Underworld – MMM Skyscraper, I Love You

The two principles of Underworld – vocalist Karl Hyde and guitarist Rick Smith – were originally in the band Freur, which had a minor synthpop hit called Doot Doot in the new wave ‘80s. A fairly pedestrian and entirely meaningless song, Doot Doot was helped immeasurably by its lush, layered production, and pointed the duo in a direction that would reach fruition ten years later.

Freur dissolved after a couple of minor albums, and Hyde and Smith formed Underworld, as a kind of industrial funk band. They released two albums (notoriously bad – especially to fans of the current incarnation) and disappeared again. Then they had the foresight or luck to invite a DJ, Darren Emerson, to join their ranks. This was a time – in the mid ‘90s – when dance music (house/techno/ whatever) had fully established itself as a vital and popular genre, and lots of people were trying to figure out ways to capitalize on it. For the most part, songs were songs and dance tracks were dance tracks and it was hard to find a common ground – outside of extended remixes of songs for use in clubs. Songs need, for the most part, a certain structure and brevity. They are meant to be listened to. Rarely can you get away with repeating the same musical phrase over and over again with very little (if any) variation without losing your audience. Dance music, on the other hand, doesn’t have an audience. Or, at least, not one that sits and listens to the music. Dance music is, duh, meant to be danced to, and the requirements are completely different. Here, long stretches of nearly identical phrases are a good thing. The music can take much longer to develop because the groove is gold. In fact, too much change in a song is bad for dancing, as it causes you to pay more attention to the song than you are to the real reason you’re there – moving and grooving and mingling with sweaty hotties. Dance tracks also tend to have really long intros and outros, to facilitate cross mixing by the DJs. Consequently, you almost never hear a cut in its entirety in a club, but it’s meant to be woven into an elaborate tapestry by the beatmaster.

So there was some trouble getting the two forms together. Some musicians chose to add house or techno elements to traditional song structures, with pretty poor results. Others, like William Orbit’s experiments with his old band Torch Song or Underworld, went the other way. They took songs and exploded them into techno tracks, leaving little fragments of the original song scattered about in service of the groove, rather than trying to shoehorn the groove into the song. When Underworld unleashed this form of exploded dance music, the reaction was swift and sure, and they became one of the legendary, seminal dance acts of the ‘90s.

This album, with the very unwieldy title of Dubnobasswithmyheadman, created a minor sensation among dance aficionados (my friend Russell, who’s eclectic tastes run from four-on-the-floor disco madness to country twang, turned me on to it), but they were really thrust into the limelight with their inclusion in a key scene (the last one) of the notorious film Trainspotting, a dark look at young Scottish heroin addicts that seduced with its sound and look while repulsing with its content – and which gave most of the world their first look at actor Ewan McGregor. That track, Born Slippy, brought Underworld to the surface, and they very quickly became the dance act of 1993 – at least in England. Two years later, Dubnobasswithmyheadman was released stateside, where it was embraced by clubheads and music lovers, but never really caught on with the larger public.

Underworld’s sound is long and dark, with punishing beats, glass-shattering guitar shards, and twisted, surreal snatches of vocals. Rumor has it that the vocalist, Karl Hyde, was an alcoholic during the early years of the breakout Underworld and wrote and performed his lyrics tanked to the gills, which is what gives them their strange, disconnected feel. He was worried, a couple of albums later, that his sobriety would affect his songwriting, but he had gotten to the point where he had to put his health first. Consequently, some of the later tracks have disjointed lyrics, but they’re kind of studied disjointed, instead of the more natural off-the-wallness that is so intriguingly surreal on this album. The meaning (and actual contents) of the lyrics have been the source of many entertaining discussions between me and my friends (porn dog? way up in my big black bank?)

Until you listen to the lyrics, such as they are, you might think MMM Skyscraper I Love You is a modernist ode to Mies Van Der Rohe and, in a way, it is. It isn’t so much a literal ode, but a figurative one. Mies was one of the most influential modernist architects of the 20th century (that’s redundant, only the 20th century is modernist), with his Bauhaus inspired “form follows function” steel and glass box skyscrapers. Although some interesting work did come out of the Bauhaus, I must confess that I hate Van Der Rohe’s ugly block buildings – which I once heard referred to as “the fuck you school of architecture”. Some of their ideas make sense – ornamentation isn’t really necessary, there is a beauty to simplicity – and some of it doesn’t (yes, a flat roof is the simplest and uses the fewest materials, but it is also the ugliest and, in many ways, the least practical (the rain and snow go where?). There is a band called Bauhaus, with their early goth classic Bela Lugosi is Dead (the lead singer of Bauhaus would hook up with ex-Japan bassist Mick Karn for a one-off project called Dali’s Car after both their bands broke up), but I wouldn’t call Bauhaus particularly Bauhausian. Underworld, however, fits the ideals perfectly. Aiming squarely for the dance floor, Underworld strips down everything that isn’t essential, which is the beat the beat the beat. Making no concessions to acoustic sounds or the natural world, Underworld is defiantly artificial in its sounds and structures. Riffs repeat over and over again, floating on top of the blur of beats, until it all runs together in a trance-inducing barrage and you are either separated from your body in an ecstatic dance of pure techno rhapsody, or you run screaming from the room, unable to take it any more. If you listen to this track and start to fall into the second camp, take a deep breath and wait a few minutes. One of the things I especially like about this track is the slow build-up and then, just when you’ve exhausted the beat, the way it shifts and changes into something lighter and airier for the last couple of minutes. And if you really can’t stand it, stop listening to it. Underworld, in addition to being great dance music and great to exercise to, works really well as ambient work music. It may seem a little too aggressive to be ambient, but if you turn it down a bit, it will carry you through your task. This is how Russell discovered it, working a 20-hour shift in an editing room, letting Underworld carry him when the caffeine ran out, and it’s one of my favorite uses for it too. It’s ignorable, but the beat keeps you from falling into a coma.

Underworld has released several albums since their scorching debut Dubnobasswithmyheadman, but I don’t like any of them as much as I like that one – and the public seems to concur. Still widely respected, their sales have fallen off dramatically – but that hardly matters to them as they are now also Tomato, a high-profile graphics design firm that does work for major international conglomerations (think Nike and Pepsi). Tomato pays the bills that Underworld can’t, and it keeps them from having to pander to the public or their label. After three albums together, DJ Emerson has split the group to pursue his own muse and Hyde and Smith have released A Hundred Days Off without him. How they fair without the presence that kick-started their career remains to be heard – at least by me. After wading through Second Toughest in the Infants (?) and Beaucoup Fish (??), I’ve tired of them. They are good at what they do, but they already did it on Dubnobasswithmyheadman and they just keep doing the same thing again. Even when the groove is good, and theirs is impeccable, the night eventually comes to an end and even the hardiest partier needs to go to bed and get some sleep.
