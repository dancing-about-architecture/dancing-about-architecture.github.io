Combustible Edison – Carnival of Souls

Throughout the 20th century, people have started to show an amazing lack of foresight and future-consciousness. I don’t know if it has to do with the rise of technology or the knowledge that we can now wipe out the earth and every living thing on it (except the cockroaches) in a matter of minutes, but people have been acting like they’re living in the last generation for some time now with disastrous results. Some are truly tragic – like the “me first” mentality that causes measures like the notorious Proposition 13 to pass in California, whereby homeowners slash their property taxes at the expense of education and social programs (“who cares what happens to the schools,” the "thinking" seems to be, “I’ve already graduated,”). Y2K, the great, enormously expensive, non-event that kicked off the millennium (or, really, the pre-millennium, for the actual millennium didn’t start until Y2K+1), entirely attributable to lack of foresight (yes, I know, the programmers were trying to save a couple of bytes of memory, but they sure weren’t thinking very far in advance). And then there have been the succession of evil Republicans in the White House who not only believe the Rapture is coming (and that they won’t be “left behind”), but actually seem to be trying to bring it on while in office.

As usual, I’m less concerned with the important issues, and more hung up on the trivialities. Like, who thought “Modern Art” was a good idea? Actually, that is a subject worthy of an entire shelf of books, but I’m not talking about the concept of a non-representational art form that no longer tries to reach a wider audience than the huddled group of elitist initiates it currently communes with, I’m just talking about the name. Modern Art. I was so incensed when, as a child, I learned that Modern Art didn’t mean current art (what has come to be known by the considerably less catchy name of contemporary art), but that it referred to a particular style from a particular span of time during the middle of the 20th century. Like, what, they thought that was going to be the end of artistic development? They didn’t think there would be anything after it? So now we are stuck in a linguistic cul-de-sac, forced to use “post-modern” (Devo has a song that points out the obvious problem with this, called Post-Post-Modern Man) or forced to make art that references past movements by slapping a “neo” in front of it (which is just another dead end – how do you distinguish neo-classicism from the 19th century with neo-classicism from the late 20th?). Soon we will be forcing our kids to endure afternoons of Neo-Post-Post-Modernist exhibits at the Relatively Current Museum for Art That is Being Done Now, More or Less.

And then there’s music. Every ten to twenty years, popular music, as it is practiced on the big market radio stations, becomes stagnant and an alternative to that kind of music suddenly starts to gain a toe-hold and becomes the big new sound, until, a few years later, that gets old and tired and some fresh new sound blows in to take over. Although, if you look at it (listen to it), it’s often just been a battle between technology and rock ‘n’ roll. Musical technology advances to a point where new sounds are available and the novelty of those sounds take over as everybody exploits them for a few years, and then people get tired of the lifeless gloss that inevitably turns into, and somebody plugs a battered guitar into an old amp, slashes out three chords with passion and no precision, and, bang, a “new” movement starts. Oh boy.

It’s actually, if I may be permitted to dust off some old high-school philosophy, a classic example of the Hegelian Dialectic in action. The thesis is in place (rock ‘n’ roll), it’s challenged by an antithesis (symphonic pop), and they duke it out for a while until they reach a synthesis (MOR AOR (that’s “Middle of the Road” “Album Oriented Rock”, for those that don’t read “the trades”)). That becomes the new thesis, which leads to a new antithesis (punk) which leads to a new synthesis (new wave) and so on and so on to wherever we are now, with the obnoxiously named “nü-metal” (punk + heavy metal + hip hop) in ascendancy, or descendancy, depending on your level of optimism.

That’s all well and good, that’s how movements and ideas (“memes”) evolve. But don’t go calling your antithesis “alternative”, because that again paints you into a linguistic corner. But, that’s exactly what happened in the early '90s. People (well, some people, anyway) got tired of the fruitful combination of technology and rock ‘n’ roll called new wave (another stupid name, like, what, there’s not going to be another wave after that?) that had lead to meticulously produced clouds of vapor, and so they plugged their guitars back into their buzzing amps and reinvented the rock ‘n’ roll wheel as an alternative to what was being played on top 40 stations across the country. So, naturally, they called this “new” music Alternative, as in something that was an alternate choice if you didn’t like the mainstream. Really, really dumb. For what happens when Alternative becomes mainstream (as it inevitably did)? Then do you have Alternative Alternative rising up in the antithesis role? Do you start playing Neo-Alternative music when the Alternative Alternative needs an alternative?

The other problem is that Alternative is essentially meaningless. It, of course, needs something to be an alternative to, but that only tells you what it isn’t, not what it is, and it only tells you what it isn’t if you already know what is, and that isn’t very helpful to anybody. It leads directly to the kind of huddled elitist initiates who argue over the minutiae of a specialized form of expression that you need to have studied for years before you begin to appreciate its subtleties which causes it to be rejected by an impatient public who just wants to hears some cool tunes/look at a pretty picture/read a fluffy book/see a movie that makes sense.

Although Alternative is now generally meant to refer to Nirvana and the Seattle scene that exploded because of it, at the time, it really encompassed all sorts of music that had nothing to do with that aesthetic, lumped together because it also wasn’t what it was being alternative to. So, the Neo-Swing revival of the mid-'90s also got a lot of play on Alternative stations, as did its more ironic cousin, space age bachelor pop or lounge music. It was only a matter of time before some group of young rapscallions would try to recreate the lounge sound with a knowing wink. That band would be Combustible Edison.

Their music is reasonably good, but it’s difficult escaping the fact that they are doing this ironically. It probably shouldn’t make any difference if the product is good enough, but somehow it does. Wink-wink, nudge-nudge irony wears pretty thin pretty quickly, and part of the appeal of the real lounge scene is its disarming sincerity.

This instrumental track is named after a well-regarded, low budget, 1962 film that, sadly, I have never seen. Carnivals are a wonderful cross breed of sleaze and fun, a meretricious world that underground artist Robert Williams (who worked in one for years) memorably refers to as “sham glorious”. This toodling calliope waltz captures both the innocent hope of glory and the creepy sleaze of reality that coexist in carnivals. The lurching drums and twangy guitar also add to the broken-down carousel-in-hell feel to the track and the wordless vocals add a nice sprinkling of exotica to the whole package. It’s a nice place to visit, but you wouldn’t want to live there.
