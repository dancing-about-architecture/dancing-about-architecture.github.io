Hapa – Ka Uluwehi O Ke Kai

My dad kept his mysterious life so compartmentalized that, whenever I’d go visit him, he’d take time from work and throw me in the car and we’d go off on some grand road trip adventure. At first, I thought this was terribly exciting, but I came to see it as a way to keep the different parts of his life separate. He was a great story teller and, with his worlds colliding, there was always the possibility that one of his stories would be proven false. When I was up in Alaska, cleaning up after his death, I was constantly asked how my sister was taking it and if she was going to come up to help.

I am an only child.

At any rate, he and I would get in the car and pick a general direction and just drive for a week or two, seeing where the road would lead us. As a consequence – and because of the fair amount of moving around I did after high school – I can take great pride in having visited 47 of the 50 United States. The three that I’ve missed? Oklahoma, Arkansas, and Hawaii. I do want to hit all 50, and I imagine making it out to Oklahoma and Arkansas will be a bit of a chore – not only because they’re out of the way, but because I can’t think of any real reason to visit other than I haven’t been there – but I’ve been dying to go to Hawaii ever since my grandmother, Oma, traveled there when I was but a wee lad. She was always off on some remarkable trip – Africa, China – and would return with a few carousels of slides (much to my mother’s chagrin), but it was Hawaii that captured my imagination. The warm seas, the tropical beaches, the lush rainforests, it all sounds too good to be true. In fact, I heard that missionaries once introduced snakes and pests and plants with thorns to the islands because they didn’t have any of their own and the repressed Catholics felt that was just too idyllic, that it really was too good to be true and it was their job to mess it up. It seems a dubious story, but worse has been done in the name of noble suffering, so who knows.

When I think of Hawaii, in addition to the surf and the sand and the undulating hula dancers and the roast pig of the luau, I also think of the kind of seductively mellow music played by Hapa. Hapa is a (an?) Hawaiian duo, consisting of a native islander and a blond beach boy (Barry Flanagan and Keli’i Kaneali’I – I’ll let you work out which is which). They celebrate the native heritage of the islands and work hard to preserve and promote the traditional music through their superbly produced tracks. This first, eponymous album, which can be a bit difficult to track down, alternates between instrumentals and songs, and showcases their rich acoustic guitar sound and smooth vocal harmonies. Some of their songs are originals, some are traditional, and some are covers – including a version of a John Lennon tune (Oh My Love) that closes the album. The title of this track (written by Edith Kanaka’ole) translates to The Plants of the Sea and is a perfect example of how an exotic tongue can make the most ridiculous lyrics sound beautiful and romantic and mysterious, such as in the second verse of this song:

He lipoa i pae i ke one

Ke one hinuhinu la

Wela i ka la ke hehi a’e

Mai mana’o he pono keia!

Which translates to:

It is lipoa which washes ashore

Onto the shiny white sand

Hot from the heating sun as you step on it

Don’t think that this is fun!

I love this album, and should go out and try to track down some of their newer releases, but I made the mistake of buying their Christmas album, Hapa Holidays a couple of years ago. It is so unbearably sincere and saccharine that I can’t stand to listen to it, and it has left an unforgivably bad taste in my mouth. Of course, that’s a common problem with Christmas albums, so maybe I should give them another chance.
