Jane Siberry – Love is Everything

Hailing from Canada like several of her more high-profile big-voiced sisters (kd lang, Celine Dion, Alanis Morrisette), Jane Siberry has followed what is apparently a very fickle muse through a spotty 20-year career. Starting out by singing in a coffeehouse while she worked her way through college and a degree in microbiology (!), she has made a career out of daring fusions of pop song structures, ethereal soundscapes, and beautifully heart-catching poetry. She’s one of those brilliant and original artists whose lot it is to toil in obscurity, lighting up the lives of but a few scattered souls who appreciate her unique talents.

The closest she’s come to popularity is on the remarkable When I Was a Boy album, which is the first time most of the world (myself included) got the chance to hear her but which was something like the 12th album of her career. Produced in part by Brian Eno and infinite guitarist Michael Brook, When I Was a Boy is a beautiful, spiritual, sensual journey through dreamy, fractured pop. It begins with one of the most enticing album openers I’ve ever heard – light percussion, hovering piano chords, and a startling, frankly erotic “mmm…gimme”.

It continues, unfolding and painting beautiful portraits and landscapes, quirky and memorable. Although it is possible to be a woman and sing songs that don’t underscore that fact, all of the songs on When I was a Boy (ironically) seem to dwell in a very powerful, very feminine place. Not the kind of fake, sugar-coated, pink tights and pigtails kind of femininity nor the twisted, sex object, hot babe kind of femininity (which is just masculinity through a prism), but the deeply powerful, darkly mysterious, baby-making Amazon femininity that can make men feel inadequate at best and entirely unnecessary at worst. It is for this reason, I believe, that it is one of my wife’s all-time favorite albums.

Siberry uses a lot of primal iconography in her songs – lots of fire and water and angels – which gives the music a sort of mythic sheen. She has a spectacular voice, and she knows how to use it well, singing intimately and honestly and saving the soaring, operatic moments for when they will be most effective. When I Was a Boy (reminds me of a Lounge Lizards track in which the only words appear near the end, in the form of a deep man’s voice (á la Tom Waits) hoarsely muttering, “when I was a little girl…”) ends with this track, a moving meditation on love. It’s hard to believe that anybody could write a new or affecting love song anymore, but she manages it gloriously, capturing both the soaring hope and the brittle delicacy of our most treasured emotion. One of the best – and rarest – things about this love song is that it isn’t a song declaring her undying devotion to somebody, it is a love song to love itself. As such, it focuses on the singer. Love is important for creating bonds to people, true, but love may be more important for what it does to the lover, not to the loved. After all, that’s where love really lives, in the heart of the one who loves.

As much as I like this track, I think my favorite song of hers is the surreal and somnabulant The Lobby. A slow-motion dream of a song, it strings together inventive production, intimate vocals, and disjointed but evocative phrases. My favorite moment is when a chorus of disapproving voices chides Jane that “this is your darkest hour” and she responds, one lone frail voice arcing up to the sky, “this is my finest moment”. It’s truly a wonderful moment in a glorious song, and I’d recommend her Love is Everything two-disc compilation, as it includes both The Lobby and Love is Everything, as well as an intriguing cross-section of her career, obscure though it may be. She has the kind of distinct style and unique vision that deserves wider appreciation but, in all likelihood, it is just that distinct uniqueness that keeps her outside the warm fires of mainstream acceptance. Oh well, Jane, you could always go back to microbiology, I suppose.
