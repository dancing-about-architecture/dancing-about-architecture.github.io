Rindé Eckert – Ellen Waltzing

Anybody who works for any amount of time in a creative discipline knows what it’s like to be possessed. To be slogging along through your book or your painting or your piece of music, and to suddenly feel the hand of god directing you to go someplace you never intended to go. Writers talk about how a well-formed character will suddenly come to life and resist all of their “creator’s” attempts to make them do and say something. When this happens, it’s always wise to let the intruding muse have her way. It’s an odd experience, to suddenly discover that you’re in the passenger seat, but if you let yourself go, you can end up in some fascinating unknown territory. I like to think this is pretty common, and explains why sometimes a wonderful musician of great skill and sensitivity can drop a dead dog into the middle of an album (not all muses are good muses, not all detours are scenic), and why someone of no recognizable talent can suddenly produce a perfectly polished gem and then slink back into the mud.

Such is the case for Rindé Eckert. Actually, that’s a bit too harsh. It isn’t that he doesn’t have any recognizable talent – he is a fine singer – but everything else on this album (which is too rare for you to worry about trying to find anyway), is so different from this one track that you wonder where this piece came from. Eckert is a performance artist, and much of the music on the album is concerned with different folk traditions and features piano and organ and accordion and, throughout, his lovely, well-trained voice. Yawn. And then comes Ellen Waltzing, a beautiful, fragile, nostalgic waltz, full of bittersweet sorrow. Its wordless humming gives it a universal feel – it could be from almost any country. It’s very cinematic, and sounds just like somebody wistfully watching young lovers twirl under the stars and thinking back to their own lost opportunities. Close-up on the tear rolling into the crease of smiling lips.

Much of what makes this track great is the otherworldly chiaroscuro guitar playing of Bill Frisell. I’ve never heard anybody coax these kinds of sounds from a guitar before. So tender, so haunting, so beautiful, so soft you want to hold your breath. Frisell is another musician who, left to his own devices, can come up with some of the most strident, grating music possible, but when softened by his muse, can produce some of the most beautiful tones I’ve ever heard come out of a guitar.
