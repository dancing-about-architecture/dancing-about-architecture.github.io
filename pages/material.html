Material – Words of Advice

There’s prolific and then there’s prolific. There’s Mozart with his 41 symphonies and then there’s Hayden with 104. There’s Ennio Morricone scoring close to 50 films a year, and then there’s Esquivel, who scored an hour-long variety show every day (got the script in the morning, broadcast in the evening). There’s the Beatles, cranking out 3 or 4 records a year and then there’s Bill Laswell churning out 30 or 40.

Bill Laswell joins Bill Nelson, Pete Namlook, and Aphex Twin’s Richard James as the most prolific recording artists of our time. But while Nelson, Namlook and James (sounds like a law firm) are bedroom knob twiddlers, wiling away the night twisting their knobs, Bill Laswell gets his juice through collaboration.

Laswell has been a cornerstone in the NYC music scene for years, and has turned his Greenpoint Studios into a veritable font of inventive musical hybridization. Akin to Peter Gabriel in his Real World Studios in England, Laswell likes to gather seemingly disparate musicians together to see what kind of synergistic alchemy can be achieved. Laswell himself is a bassist by trade, and his thunderous silky sound can be heard on most releases that come out of Greenpoint. Drunk on the power of punk and the flow of funk, Laswell revels in the interfaces between different musical styles and idioms. Jazz, rap, soul, funk, Irish, ambient, you name it, the mad doctor of Brooklyn has crossbred all of them – some to magnificent effect.

One of his core groups is (or was, haven’t heard much from them lately) the Material collective. More a buffet of musical guests rotating around Laswell then a proper group, Material was formed in 1979 and featured some of the earliest interest in adapting and adopting third world music into popular Western conventions, and which also, curiously enough, featured a nascent Whitney Huston long before she became the queen diva she is today. An on again off again proposition for years, Material released its strongest album in 1994, The Hallucination Engine. Throwing everything possible into the mix, The Hallucination Engine walks the fine line between interesting polycultural experimentation and muddled musical mess, and is able to largely steer clear of the pitfalls. It’s remarkable how well jazz solos sit on top of Indian ragas, or how natural heavy metal guitars sound with tribal drumming. Or how cool funk sounds with the wizard of junk croaking out his hard-won life lessons.

William S. Burroughs, America’s coolest old guy, rose to notorious fame by writing the grotesquely surreal cult classic, Naked Lunch – which, I must point out, is almost unreadable. I’ve tried twice and haven’t been able to do it – although I’m sure his fans and aficionados would attribute that to my weak character instead of to his muddled writing. Of course it’s muddled, the guy was a junkie. Burroughs is one of those mythical American underworld literary icons – like Kerouac or Ginsberg – and much of his fame seems to come from the fact that he survived his life as long as he did (he died in 1997 at 83). Originator of the cut-up technique of verbal collage, wife killer, and sometimes closet homosexual, Burroughs lived a lifestyle that hadn’t been invented yet, a rock and roller before his time (and legion are the rockers who tip their hats to him, whether by adopting his prose fragments as their name (Steely Dan, Soft Machine) or by praising him effusively or eagerly collaborating with him on his spoken word adventures (Nirvana, Laurie Anderson)).

So you figure, someone who’s been around as many blocks as Burroughs would have some good words of advice. And indeed he does. This track is basically a mellow funk groove backing up his trademark creaky voice rattling off a few things he’s learned over the years. His advice is both hysterical and profound, sacred and profane, and the backing groove, with occasional jazz noodling and the weird tape machine noises Laswell favors, is a perfect compliment. And his advice is spot on.

Beware of whores who say they don’t want money.

The hell they don’t.

What they mean is they want more.

Much more.

Never interfere in a boy and girl fight

And my personal favorite,

If you’re doing business with a religious son-of-a-bitch,

Get it in writing.

His word isn’t worth shit,

Not when the good lord taught him how

To fuck you out of a deal.

It’s no wonder he’s so popular to quote. I often find myself using a line from this track when I’m forced to spend a lot of money on something

Who wants to be the richest guy in some cemetery?

Alas, Burroughs is gone, but Laswell lives on, pumping out the jams and mixing musical modes together. One of the best ways in to his musical universe is to get one of the 2-disc compilations his Axiom label occasionally puts out. Axiom Ambient, Axiom Dub, and Axiom Funk are all worth a listen.
