United Future Organization – Bar-f-out

Okay, here’s my dirty little secret – or one of them, anyway. I don’t like jazz. I’m not proud of this, like I am of my distaste for Led Zeppelin or the Rolling Stones. I wish I liked jazz, and I like the story of jazz and how it developed. I could speak fairly knowledgeably about Louis Armstrong and Harlem rent parties and the birth of bop and the king of swing, but when it comes right down to it, I don’t actually listen to jazz much at all. For which I am embarrassed.

Some of it is because of its blues roots. I really don’t like the blues for very long and any music that uses that as its foundation tends to bore me. But there’s more to it than that. Really what I don’t like is the culture of the solo. I don’t like to sit for twenty minutes and listen to a saxophone unspool a solo, even if it is extemporaneous and musically sophisticated. And it isn’t just jazz, I’m equally bored listening to a blistering guitar solo. I’m much more interested in the interlocking of musical parts, which is why I was so fascinated by new wave’s interest in African music and the communal nature of that kind of musical construction. The me-first, look at my big swinging dick nature of trading solos and musical one-upmanship that has, fairly or not, become the standard jazz paradigm is of no interest to me. At least from a listening point of view. When visiting my brother-in-law in New Orleans one weekend, I was treated to a night out listening to jazz at Snug Harbor. I could barely conceal my unenthusiasm, but was pleasantly surprised by the set. Watching jazz is something else entirely, because I could follow the threads as they were being passed around and could study each musician’s reaction to what the others were doing and it made for a very entertaining evening out. All of those layers of interwoven communication are invisible on a recording and I just don’t care enough about somebody’s sound or modalities to want to delve into many jazz recordings. This, I take pains to note, is nothing about jazz. Jazz is good and jazz is great, a symbol for freedom and perhaps the greatest American contribution to world culture. The fault lies entirely within me. My soul is too small and impoverished to really appreciate the beautiful tapestry of jazz.

So, this is about as close as I get which, admittedly, isn’t very close at all. Acid jazz, the mixing of jazz idioms with electronica, is usually more about acid than jazz, and that suits me just fine. And United Future Organization is easily the best acid jazz combo out there, despite the fact that you’ve never heard of them. UFO is a collaboration between two Japanese and one French DJ, and their cross-cultural take on electronica and jazz enlivens both considerably. In fact, jazz is generally better regarded in both Europe and Japan than it is back home in the States, so what may seem an odd pedigree for an acid jazz outfit actually makes lots of sense.

One of the best things about UFO, evident on this track, is their inventive use of sampling. This track is built around an odd bite from the primitive string quartets John Lurie wrote for Jim Jarmusch’s wonderful Stranger Than Paradise. They compile wonderful moody music, going from slick late-night noir soundtracks like The Moving Shadows (in which they take a bite out of Mel Brooks’ Young Frankenstein (!)) to five on the floor rave ups (The Planet Plan, from their 3rd Perspective album, swings so confidently that it took a couple of dozen listens for me to realize that it was in 5/4 – no mean feat!). They swing, they sway, they cut and they mix, and they bring new skills and shades to the concept of what jazz is and what it can be. Purists would turn green, I’m sure, but I think this counts as jazz – it’s got that swing, so it must mean a thing, even Louis would agree.
