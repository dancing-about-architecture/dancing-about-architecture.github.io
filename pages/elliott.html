Dean Elliott – The Lonesome Road

One day, the story goes, bandleader Dean Elliott was sitting in his convertible at a stoplight. Next to him was a cement truck, slowly churning away. The rhythm was precise and regular and Elliott soon found himself snapping his fingers and nodding along. Inspired, he rushed home and started working on Zounds, What Sounds!, a magnificent album of what might be called big band concrete. Not concrete like what was being mixed in the truck that sparked the imagination of Elliott, but concrete as in musique concrete, the movement that worked to incorporate the sounds of the world into the rarified air of the concert hall. Musique concrete would never catch on in more than a purely academic way, and it would be decades before it reached its fruition with sample-happy groups like the Art of Noise, but Dean Elliott is an interesting – and virtually unknown – link in the story.

Elliott uses anything and everything he can get his hands on to embellish the sound of the band, so much so that on the record, they’re credited as Dean Elliott and his Big Big Band (bigger than just big). On this record, you can hear trees falling, bowling balls crashing into pins, gargling, the staccato click of high-heels on pavement, and almost anything else that makes an unusual noise – including the kitchen sink. A novelty record to be sure, but if you listen past the chainsaws and steam whistles, you’ll hear wonderful arrangements of classic tunes. Even without the very appealing and well-integrated sound effects, the arrangements sparkle and glisten in the more-than-capable hands (and mouths) of the band. And remember, this was originally released in the early '60s, which means no multi-tracking, which means virtually everything would have to have been played and recorded live.

During the few minutes between my discovery of what came to be known as space age bachelor pad music and the rest of the world’s discovery of it (and my corresponding cooling towards it), this was my favorite album. I originally knew about it from a tape that a friend of cartoonist Wayno had given me – I don’t know why cartoonists tended to champion this music, but there’s a very strong connection between the two, for some reason. Surprisingly, this gem was actually released later in a limited edition on CD, and I’m grateful that I was one of the thousand that had the opportunity to buy it. As far as space age bachelor pad music goes, Esquivel is the king, hands down. But Dean Elliott is the son and Perrey & Kingsley the holy ghost, and of the three, I am more likely to want to listen to Dean. It must be because his band wasn’t just big, it was big big.
