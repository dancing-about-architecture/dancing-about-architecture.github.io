DJ Shadow – Stem

I long ago gave up trying to divide my collection into genres, although I did have a classical ghetto until fairly recently – but that had less to do with the fact that classical doesn’t belong with the other musical forms (it does) or because it’s easy to tell what’s classical and what isn’t (it’s not), but because of the annoying tendency of labels to release classical albums with multiple composers on the same disc – keeping them separate meant that I had fewer discs to look through when I forgot that Smetana was paired up with Liszt, or that that Purcell disc also has a couple of killer Schöenberg tracks. So now, except for my compilations, I just keep everything alphabetical. But even that isn’t as clean as I would like. Against official advise, I tend to ignore spaces in alphabetizing, so The Beat Nuts comes after The Beatles, even though it should go before. Pretty minor, I realize, but a bigger problem is what to do about bands like 3rd Bass or 2Badcard or 808 State. Although Strunk & White probably demand that they be placed at the beginning of the alphabet because they begin with numerals, I choose to file them as though the numerals were spelled out. Not kosher, I know, but much easier for me to deal with.

A bigger problem, since I separate my compilations, is what to do about discs like the soundtrack for The Crying Game, which is half songs by various artists and half music from the film by Anne Dudley (formerly of The Art of Noise). Or the Yellow Submarine soundtrack, which is half Beatles songs and half George Martin compositions. And it’s well established that you are to ignore articles in titles, so The Butthole Surfers gets filed under B instead of T. But does that hold for other languages as well? Do you file Los Lobos under Los or Lobos? Okay, that’s a trick question – unless you’ve got a Lobsterboy or Lobotomy album, it doesn’t matter which word you file it under, it’ll go in the same place – like my dilemma about filing the soundtrack to Purple Rain under Purple or Prince. But Les Negresses Vertes? El Chicano? Un Drame Musical Instantane? What’s a librarian to do? And where should one put DJ records? Is “DJ” part of their name, or is it a title, like Mr.? Should I file DJ Facehole next to DJ Spooky or not? Actually, in that case, I’d say DJ is a title, but I still file all of them next to each other under D. It just makes it easier to find, and that’s the ultimate criterium of a good filing system, Strunk & White be damned.

Brian Eno said that in the future, the artist wouldn’t be a creator so much as a curator. Well, the future is now. Collage/montage is one of the most effective ways of dealing with the flood of informational overload and dovetails nicely with post-modernism’s tenets of recycling and recontextualization. It has long been practiced in art and, to a lesser degree, literature, and the sampler has brought the recontextualization of music into the mainstream. And almost nobody has done more creative sample manipulation and juxtoposition than DJ Shadow, a white kid from California. While others, like Beats International/Fatboy Slim mastermind Norman Cook or De La Soul’s producer Prince Paul have a similarly catholic taste in music and adventurousness in sampling, DJ Shadow (aka Josh Davis) follows those ideas out farther than almost anybody else will go (a notable exception being John Oswald and his ongoing – and perpetually litigated – Plunderphonic experiments – and lots of luck finding any of those at your local Tower Records). Dropping the strict reliance on dance friendly grooves that confine most other sample-happy producers, DJ Shadow will gladly follow his muse wherever she leads him, no matter how dark and foreboding the terrain might be.

DJ Shadow’s first full-length release, Endtroducing, was all the rage a couple of years ago, with critics falling all over themselves to praise him for pushing DJ culture into uncharted territory and heralded his moody collages as a bold and brave new world for samplers and mixers. One of the best things on it is this track. I love the soundtracky feel to the beginning, the way it adds subtle strings and bells to the repeating harp motif. I also like the way the drums slowly assert themselves, and then suddenly burst into a full-on frantic, kinetic drum ‘n’ bass groove, before stopping short with some snatches of voices giving fragments of directions. Then the harp picks up again, accompanied by what sounds like a cello articulating the theme. A couple more moments of tension, a sliver of a vocal, then the drums start again, getting louder and faster and more frantic, layers quickly being added, tension mounting, strings swirling, higher and higher, faster and faster, until it climaxes with a nagging, repeating high string flourish.

As a DJ release, it is startlingly unlike anything else any other DJ is doing. Unfortunately, it is also startlingly unlike anything else he does on the rest of this album.

My morning DJ of choice for years was KCRW’s venerable Chris Douridas, former captain of the legendary Morning Becomes Eclectic show. Towards the end of his tenure, Douridas started branching out into other areas in the music business – advising Dreamworks Records, consulting for film soundtracks – which eventually led to charges of conflict of interest and his eventual resignation (not that one necessarily led to the other). He used to particularly bug me in his capacity as a music consultant for soundtracks, as he would regularly turn producers on to music that was too cool for their film and taint, slightly, my image of that music. So we get a great soundtrack to a film like Michael Mann’s Heat which, unfortunately, has nothing to do with what’s on the screen. Likewise, he consulted for Desperate Measures, and because of that, a dog of a film gets to have this song and another one of my most cherished pieces of music, David Darling’s In November, on its soundtrack. Unfortunately, what such a stunt does is just point out how much better than the movie the music is, degrading both. So remember, if you’re going to make a bad movie, for god’s sake, use bad music!
