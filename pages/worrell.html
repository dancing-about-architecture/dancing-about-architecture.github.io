Bernie Worrell – At Mos’ Spheres

Bernie Worrell was one of the principle passengers on the Mothership. There’s funk, and then there’s funk, and Worrell was one of the main funkmeisters of all time, although he isn’t usually given the recognition that his more flamboyant partners, bassist Bootsy Collins and master of funk disaster George Clinton get. Clinton formed two groups that worked and recorded simultaneously (and shared members) during the ‘70s and ‘80s, Parliament and Funkadelic. Funkadelic tackled a more rock-oriented take on funk, while Parliament was a completely out of control funk collective, folding psychedelic acid rock, science fiction, comic book culture and some of the most outrageous costumes imaginable into their batter. They also had some of the most intriguingly bizarre album covers of all time, with elaborate drawings with insane amounts of details and in jokes and innuendo and completely nonsensical stories about coming from outer space to free the funk.

Bernie Worrell is a classically trained musician, studying piano from the age of three and writing his first concerto at the age of eight (take that, Wolfie). His training allowed him to give a shape and scope to Clinton’s mad funk vision and his horn charts and keyboard playing largely made the P-Funk sound what it was. His trademark thin and reedy synthesizer sound has made a comeback in recent years as primarily West Coast rappers reference their old-school godfathers by sampling his keyboard lines, or recreating his sound for their own tracks.

In addition to his pivotal role in the P-Funk collective (or, as George Clinton sometimes calls is, the Parlifunkadelicment Thang), Worrell also joined Talking Heads and toured with them as part of their expanded 11-piece Remain in Light line-up, giving ultra-white guy David Byrne a few pointers on letting the funk out.

This track is taken from his album Funk of Ages, which includes an impressive list of contributors, including David Byrne, Phoebe Snow, Herbie Hancock, Bill Laswell, Bootsy Collins and Sly Dunbar, amongst others. This track, with its nonsensical pun name, is unlike anything else on the album, which is pretty straight up funk. I love the way At Mos’ Spheres highlights the two threads of Worrell’s musical life, clearly showing both his classical training and his down and dirty funk playing. He gets an amazing array of sounds out of a simple electric organ, alternating between light and delicate – almost baroque – and juke-joint nasty. It’s a tour-de-force performance, and one of my favorite solo keyboard compositions.
