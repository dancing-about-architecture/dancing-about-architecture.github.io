SPK – Doctrine of Eternal Ice

SPK was an early industrial outfit that tried as hard as possible (too hard, in some cases) to be shocking for its own sake. It was formed by two men in Germany who met at a mental hospital – one was a worker there, the other was a patient. They formed a group that quickly gained notoriety for their shocking shows. Brain-pummeling crashes cascaded over frighteningly brutal shrieks and a cacophonous wall of metallic percussion while slides of surgery and flamethrowers leapt out at the audience. SPK kept the name consistent, but what it was an abbreviation for changed from release to release. Initially they were the Sozialistische Patienten Kollektiv, a tribute to a group that advocated rights for metal patients, but it has also stood for System Planning Korporation and Surgical Penis Klinik.

SPK underwent a drastic stylistic change with the addition of multi-instrumentalist Graeme Revell and vocalist Sinan (Leong), who eventually became the only members of the group. With Revell’s addition, the group started moving towards a kinder, gentler sort of industrial mayhem and started adding more ethnic and esoteric elements into the mix, to the disgust of their early fans and their bleeding brains. But this is about when I jumped onto the SPK boat, so I find their early stuff hard to listen to.

Graeme Revell was deep into esoterica. He released a few albums of extremely arcane music under his own name – such as one made entirely of sounds sampled from insects (not as interesting as you might hope) and another making a heroic effort to translate famous crazy guy and “outsider” artsit Adolf Wölfli’s elaborate musical and pictorial fantasies into recorded sound. Again, generally not as interesting as one might hope.

Under the banner of SPK, Graeme tempered his most outrageous arcana with a dance beat in a nod to commercialism, so we get such oddly named releases as my favorite album of theirs, Digitalis Amigua, Gold & Poison (what?) and their big hit, Zamia Lehmanni: Songs of Byzantine Flowers (huh?). Actually, Digitalis Ambigua, Gold & Poison marked another turning point for the group in general and Revell in particular. The first half is largely ignorable dance-oriented music. There is one great track (Pure Naked Aggression), which utilizes the criminally underused kettle drums, but the other songs are woefully bad. Then the album takes a sharp turn and everything else on it explores a much more interesting and varied sound that previews Revell’s next career, that of film composer. He still peppers his tracks with oddness and obsolescence (to use Manhattan Transfer’s approximation of a Rod Serling line), but the ambient/ethnic sound is much more interesting than either the early noise or the later dance-schlock. Some of the standouts include White Island, which uses narration from an old newsreel about the testing of atomic bombs on South Pacific islands to a moving effect, and this track, in which the pretentious title does actually sort of fit the track, which sounds like something accompanying a doomed army’s trek across the frozen white tundra (similar to Lene Lovich’s album closing The Freeze, from Flex).

Graeme Revell, who was born in New Zealand, came to wider public notice with his award-winning score for the Australian thriller, Dead Calm, which introduced the world at large to Nicole Kidman as well. After that, he disbanded what was left of SPK and focused on scoring films and producing soundtracks. Some of his credits include Hard Target, The Crow, and Until the End of the World, Wim Wenders’ epicly long film, which my friend Russell calls Until the End of the Movie.
