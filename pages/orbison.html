Roy Orbison – Blue Angel

I thought David Lynch made Roy Orbison up. Halfway through Blue Velvet, the terrifying Frank Booth helps initiate Jeffrey into the twisted, sadistic world of adult dreams and desires by taking him to a roadhouse to see his “suave” friend Dean. Dean puffs a cigarette in a long holder, punches Jeffrey in the stomach and sings “a candy-colored clown they call the sandman” into a light bulb. No way is that a real song, I thought.

Then when I saw Roy Orbison perform on TV, I was sure David Lynch had made him up. Here was an old man, dressed entirely in black, wearing dark shades and what had to be a Beatles wig circa 1964, belting out strange cha-cha inspired songs with a voice of unearthly power and range. No way was he real, I thought.

I was wrong.

Roy Orbison hit his stride in the ‘50s, when the meat of his material was released. Best known for Pretty Woman and Only the Lonely, his catalogue of melodramatic hits range from ridiculous (the dreadful Leah, in which the singer drowns for his love) to the sublime (the aforementioned In Dreams, in which the candy-colored clown leads the sleeping singer to the arms of his love). His best songs start of restrained and quickly accelerate to unbelievable heights of passion, writhing and crashing, drums pounding, strings swelling, and soaring over all of it, the frightening power of Roy’s voice cursing the human condition with a great wailing and gnashing of teeth.

Because of its naked emotion and wild histrionics, Orbison’s music makes a great soundtrack to adolescence. That is, I think, one of the reasons Lynch used his music so effectively in his film – many of Lynch’s signs and symbols relate back to those terrifying and exhilarating days marking the passage from childhood to adulthood and all the attendant exaggerated emotions that accompany the transition. In fact, Lynch is largely – if not wholly – responsible for the exhuming of Orbison’s career just as it was coming to a close. This recording is from a set of Orbison’s best (or, at least, best-known) songs, rerecorded and produced by Lynch himself and repackaged to take advantage of his new post-Blue Velvet popularity. Purists hate the set because, by rerecording, it tampers with the holy gospel of Orbison by meddling with already perfect songs. I’m sure I would feel stronger about it if I knew the original recordings before I heard the rerecordings, but I didn’t and now the originals sound flat and unconvincing to me. In the thirtysomething years between the two recordings, Orbison lost none of his voice’s stunning range or raw emotional power, quite a feat for someone half a century past puberty.

As a result of this sudden popularity (probably boosted by the depressingly popular Pretty Woman film), Orbison got to ride the popularity wave again right before his death, releasing an album of new material and joining the supergroup Travelling Wilburys (also featuring Tom Petty, Bob Dylan, Jeff Lynne, and George Harrison) for their first (and only recommendable) album.
